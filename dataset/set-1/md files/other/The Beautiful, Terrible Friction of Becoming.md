# The Beautiful, Terrible Friction of Becoming

Coming out as a trans girl—living under a name I now keep close to my chest for my own safety—is a challenge that defies simple description. For many of us, the journey is defined by a constant fear of family backlash and the uphill battle of explaining the non-binary and trans gender spectrum to those who refuse to see it.

My transition began in the relatively "low-friction" period following my Class 10 boards. While the world around me seemed still, I was exhausted by the silent struggle of being misgendered and the internal pressure of becoming the girl I knew I was.

### The First Steps and the "Wait"

I started by telling my older sister and a few supportive peers. I hoped to be taken seriously, and in many ways, they did. Yet, I was frequently met with the phrase, **"Wait until you’re 18."** Those words stung. They made me feel like an "incomplete project"—someone whose identity was on hold, a problem to be managed rather than a person to be heard.

### Vulnerability Met with Dismissal

Talking to my parents was the hardest part. I remember the evening I told my mother; I laid my soul bare, only to be met with: *"You’re joking,"* or *"You aren't serious."* To be that vulnerable and have your truth treated as a prank is devastating. "Support" began to feel like an empty word. The social isolation that followed hurt more than I can put into words.

When I told my father via message, I was met with more resistance. While he seemed to take the news seriously, he didn't grasp the gravity of my needs. I felt invisible. I was met with guilt-tripping comments like, *"We did so much for a boy,"* as if my existence were a debt I hadn't repaid. Even worse was the interference of third parties trying to block my access to gender-affirming care and trusted specialists like Dr. Ranjita Biswas (under Sappho for Equality). It is a deep betrayal to feel untrusted by your own family regarding your own body.

### The Struggle for Affirmation

My experience with professional "help" was equally damaging. A family therapist attempted to "bring back the boy" by isolating me from the internet and ignoring my truth. This lack of affirmation led me to dark places where I felt the urge to harm myself just to cope with the erasure.

Today, I still wonder if my family will ever truly "get" it, or if they will continue to police me under the guise of "support" while blaming me for the distance between us.

### Final Thoughts

I am sharing this because the world needs to know how much families and outside sources can impact a person when they finally decide to be vulnerable. Coming out isn't just a moment; it's a navigation through a world that often tries to stop you from being yourself. Thank you for listening to my story.
