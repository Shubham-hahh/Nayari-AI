# Spending My First Day At Indian Public School (IPS) 

My first day at Indian Public School (IPS) was a dizzying blur of new beginnings and the heavy ghosts of the past. Before the first "hello" could even be whispered, the day began with the familiar, ritualistic struggle: the bathroom mirror. The morning was thick with a quiet, heavy sadness as I faced the task of shaving away a beard that felt less like hair and more like a mask I was forced to wear.

I carry this grief every morning, a constant questioning of why I remain tethered to a body that feels like a misinterpretation. I want so desperately to align my physical self with the girl who lives within me, but as I navigate the labyrinthine hurdles of the Transgender Persons Act of 2026, the path to feminizing HRT and a full social transition feels like an arduous, vertical climb.

Yet, amidst the dysphoria, I have finally found a label that feels like a sanctuary: *non-binary trans girl*.

The world demands I choose a side, insisting on the rigid binary of "male" or "female" boxes. But I have realized that I exist in the beautiful, complex space between. There is a feminine part of me that feels as delicate as silk, yet she possesses the strength of steel for simply standing her ground. My transness provides a unique, non-binary lens through which I view the world—a perspective that refuses to rely on antiquated, dusty rules. Using my chosen name and embracing this identity makes the world feel slightly more aligned, even if the rest of society is still lagging miles behind.

The morning of that first day, I walked down a peaceful road, the air still cool before the heat of the day took hold. I made my way toward the auto, my mind a whirlpool of hope and apprehension. The school was relatively new, and I clung to the hope that a fresh start might mean a kinder environment, even though the presence of my father added a layer of traditional expectation to the morning.

We arrived early to find the gates barred. A small crowd had gathered, a restless energy simmering among those waiting for the locks to turn. I noticed another boy there with his father; we fell into a hushed conversation to pass the time. He was from the ICSE board and mentioned that their results were already out. A cold spike of anxiety hit me. I wondered if I would pass, or if I was simply another cog in an education system designed to produce obedient robots rather than thinking, feeling individuals. When the doors finally creaked open, I took a deep, shaky breath and stepped into the maw of the school.

The social atmosphere was, almost immediately, a nightmare. The "boy" box I had tried so hard to escape was waiting for me. During the morning assembly, I tried to stand where I belonged, but I was physically kicked out of the girls' line by teachers and shunted back into the "male" queue. My brain was a cacophony of silent screams, begging for the darkness to swallow the humiliation. Walking to class felt like walking to a gallows; it was one of the most profoundly hurtful experiences I have ever endured.

While the routine of the assembly and the waiting felt mundane to others, being treated as a "man" by my classmates was a soul-crushing weight. I looked for a supportive environment, a place where I could simply be the woman I am, but instead, I found judgment. I saw a familiar face in the hallway and felt a brief flicker of hope, but it was extinguished when they, too, defaulted to treating me like a man.

In class, the introductions were standard, yet the air was thick with confusion regarding my preferred name. My Political Science teacher, perhaps trying to be careful but missing the mark, defaulted to "they/them" pronouns. I had to find the courage to speak up through the exhaustion, clarifying that I use "she/her."

As a member of the LGBTQ+ community, this constant exclusion is draining. The mental gymnastics required for something as simple as using a restroom—knowing I am a girl but fearing the world’s reaction—is a relentless struggle. In those moments of isolation, my mind occasionally screams for an escape, a way to simply vanish from the pain. It is a deep, visceral hurt, but I am learning to transform that pain into words. I am working on expressing my reality, telling the world that I am a girl, and reclaiming my narrative as I navigate my transition in a world that is still learning how to see me.
