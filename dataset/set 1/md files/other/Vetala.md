# Vetala

**Scientific name**: Vetala *śāvavāsin*

**Common name**: Vetala

**Earliest Ancestor**: Plague victims in Rāvana Desh

**Type**: Undead Archivists

**Diet**: **Ancestral offerings**—specifically memory-infused rice cakes, Ākṣara-charged libations, and the **unspoken silence** left at śmaśāna grounds. These must be prepared with ritual precision; impure offerings cause temporary memory loss and physical instability.

**Intelligence**: Depends on the environment of the organism

**Appearance**: Smooth, hairless skin of ashen-grey that shimmers faintly with latent Ākṣara energy, giving a corpse-like pallor. Fully human in form but gaunt and angular in build, standing 1.7m(minimum)-1.9m(maximum) tall. Eyes emit a faint golden-violet luminescence visible in dim light. Their hair manifests as wispy tendrils of shadowy energy that drift like smoke, each strand storing a fragment of ancestral memory. They adorn themselves with polished human bone ornaments carved with mantra, sacred rudraksha beads, and ritualistic funerary ash markings on their skin that shift to reflect the stories they currently carry.

**Behavior**: Varies

**Culture**: Varies across lands; centered in cremation guilds and ancestor cults

**Age**: 120-180 years (approx.)

**Backstory**: In the 87th year after "The Origin," the necropolis-city of Rāvana Desh attempted mass preservation of plague victims through Ākṣara channeling. The ritual transcended death, transforming the dying into **living repositories**—beings sustained entirely by the stories and memories that would otherwise be lost. These first Vetalas retreated to śmaśāna grounds not from shame, but because they discovered they could only **anchor** their existence in places where death is honored. They established the **Necropolitan Libraries**, vast archives where bone-carved walls bear mantras only Vetala eyes can read. They serve as **mnemonic bridges**, translating fragmented echo-impressions of the deceased into histories for the living. Their neutrality stems from reverence: they carry memories of every side's dead, and to choose would be to erase part of the record they exist to preserve.

**Reproduction**: No sexual reproduction; new Vetalas manifest when a being dies with **unresolved narrative**—a story incomplete, a truth unspoken, a history in danger of vanishing. The transformation is **voluntary** but costly: the candidate must **surrender their name and living identity**, becoming a nameless archive. The 40-day ritual (guided by elder Vetalas) teaches existence as pure memory-carrier. The 70% failure rate reflects those who cannot bear this weight—most dissolve into true death, while a tragic few become **Bhrānta-Vetala**, mindless echoes haunting śmaśāna grounds.

**Powers**: Can access Ākṣara energy directly through semi-corporeal form, granting ability to read residual Ākṣara impressions on objects and locations. High mental fortitude and resistance to psychic influences. **Mnemonic Resonance**: By touching an object, they can "hear" its emotional history, but this causes **psychic fatigue** that requires hours of meditation to recover from.

**Special Powers**: **Corpus Inhabitation**—May temporarily inhabit and animate corpses of the recently deceased (within 7 days) for up to **7 days**, but the form decays rapidly and possesses only basic motor functions. The purpose is **witness**, not utility—the Vetala retraces the dead's final steps to understand their unspoken story. **Echo Communion**—Can converse with fragmented impressions of the deceased, but responses are cryptic fragments, not answers, and each attempt risks **psychic contamination** (temporary confusion of identity). They suffer gradual dissolution in direct sunlight (losing substance hour by hour) and must return to śmaśāna grounds every **full moon cycle** to "re-anchor" or begin fading. Sacred Ākṣara wards bind them to śmaśāna grounds not as prisoners, but as **contracts they willingly sign**, choosing to anchor where memory is sacred.

**Inspiration**: Vetala from Vetala Panchavimsati and medieval Indian occult traditions, reimagined as memory-keepers rather than mere revenants.
